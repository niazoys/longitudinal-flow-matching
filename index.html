Abstract

Generative models for sequential data often struggle with sparsely sampled and high-dimensional trajectories, typically reducing the learning of dynamics to pairwise transitions. We propose Interpolative Multi-Marginal Flow Matching (IMMFM), a framework that learns continuous stochastic dynamics jointly consistent with multiple observed time points. IMMFM employs a piecewise-quadratic interpolation path as a smooth target for flow matching and jointly optimizes drift and a data-driven diffusion coefficient, supported by a theoretical condition for stable learning. This design captures intrinsic stochasticity, handles irregular sparse sampling, and yields subject-specific trajectories. Across synthetic benchmarks and real longitudinal neuroimaging cohorts, IMMFM improves both forecasting quality and downstream clinical utility.

An integrated storyline: from sparse observations to reliable trajectories

The key challenge is not just predicting the next frame—it is learning an entire continuous trajectory from sparse, irregular observations while respecting uncertainty. IMMFM addresses this in three coupled steps:

  • Step 1 · Multi-marginal path design: a piecewise-quadratic conditional path replaces chained linear transitions, avoiding discontinuous velocity between trajectory segments.
  • Step 2 · Joint drift + uncertainty learning: the model learns both deterministic flow (drift) and a data-driven diffusion coefficient tied to predictive residuals.
  • Step 3 · Clinically grounded forecasting: the learned dynamics generate subject-specific trajectories that remain stable over horizon and useful in downstream diagnosis tasks.
Core intuition: IMMFM separates smooth trend modeling (quadratic path) from volatile local uncertainty (learned diffusion), making long-range trajectory generation both tractable and robust.

Main contributions and headline results

  • Methodological contribution: piecewise-quadratic conditional path with analytically tractable flow/score targets for multi-marginal matching.
  • Stochastic modeling contribution: learned diffusion coefficient with theoretical support for stable joint drift-diffusion optimization.
  • Empirical contribution: improved forecasting over baselines on synthetic and real datasets, with especially strong gains on noisy/stochastic cohorts.

On disease progression datasets, IMMFM variants report consistent improvements in Dice, PSNR, and SSIM, while reducing clinically important geometric errors (Hausdorff distance). On ADNI, trajectory-forecasted features improve AD-vs-CN classification from 71.7% at ~18 months to 80.8% at ~36 months (+9.1%), effectively enabling earlier correct classification.

Often-missed insights from the appendix

The appendix contains key evidence for why IMMFM works—not only that it works. We highlight three under-discussed findings that strengthen the story:

  • Ablation clarity: each proposed component contributes measurably. The quadratic path gives the largest gains (up to +3.7% Dice and +2.0 dB PSNR), while learned diffusion is especially important in the stochastic GBM setting (e.g., >6.2 px Hausdorff reduction).
  • Temporal generalization: performance degrades gradually with horizon, but dataset difficulty matters: ADNI and MS degrade moderately (~3–4% Dice over ~3 years), whereas GBM degrades more sharply (~13%), exposing where uncertainty handling is most needed.
  • 3D scaling matters: IMMFM generalizes from 2D to volumetric forecasting, improving ADNI and GBM metrics and reducing inter-subject variability, showing better consistency across patients.
